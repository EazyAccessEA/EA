# EazyAccess
## A Letter to Anyone Who Builds, Buys, or Relies on Software

---

Most strategy documents begin by telling you what the company is.

This one begins with what happened to the things you used to love.

You had a product — a piece of software, a service, an app — that made some part of your life genuinely easier. It did what it said it would do. It did not try to do more. Then, at some point, you noticed it getting worse. The interface changed in ways that confused you. Features you relied on disappeared or moved behind a higher-priced tier. Advertisements appeared. Your data started being used in ways you had not agreed to. Eventually you stopped using it, or you stayed because you had no choice, or — most commonly — because the alternative didn't exist yet.

That deterioration was not an accident, and it was not caused by incompetence.

It was the inevitable consequence of a company that was built to be sold.

Venture capital funds expect returns within defined windows. The most common path to those returns is acquisition or public offering. Both require growth metrics that prioritise valuation over user experience. The moment an exit becomes the operating objective, every product decision becomes instrumental — not toward building something excellent, but toward building something that looks excellent long enough to be acquired.

This has happened so consistently across so many categories of software that we now accept it as natural. We use the word "pivot" for what is really abandonment. We use "sunset" for what is really deletion. We say a product was "acquired" when what we mean is that it was consumed by a company that wanted its users and had no further use for the product itself.

We do not accept this as natural. We think it is a structural failure — not a moral failure, not a failure of the individual founders, but the predictable output of a business model that was never designed to produce excellent software. It was designed to produce returns for investors.

EazyAccess was designed for something different.

## I. What We Refuse to Do

We will not take venture capital.

Not now. Not at a valuation that sounds flattering. Not from a partner who seems to understand what we are building. The money is not the problem. The structure is. The structure introduces a clock, and the clock changes everything.

We will not optimise for exit.

If you work at EazyAccess, your job is to build the best version of the product in your care. Not the version that looks best in an acquisition due diligence process. Not the version with the highest short-term engagement metrics. The best version. The one that people will still be grateful for in ten years.

We will not sell user data.

This is stated plainly because the industry has conditioned people to read privacy policies rather than believe them. We have no business model that involves your data. Our business model involves charging a fair price for useful software. That is the entirety of it.

We will not sell our products.

This requires some explanation, because "we will never sell" is easy to say and frequently untrue.

We mean it structurally. The legal and operational architecture of EazyAccess is designed around permanent ownership. There is no shareholder pressure to realise a return. There is no investor whose fund closes in four years. There is no performance incentive tied to an acquisition event. Permanent is not a marketing claim. It is the actual design of the business.

## II. What We Are

EazyAccess is a permanent capital vehicle for British software.

That sentence requires unpacking because each word is doing work.

*Permanent* distinguishes us from every venture-backed company, every PE rollup, and every founder who is working toward a day when they hand it to someone else. We have no exit horizon. The products we build or acquire today, we will be running, improving, and responsible for in fifteen years.

*Capital vehicle* means we are in the business of allocating resources — our own, generated entirely from product revenue — toward software businesses that can be made better and held forever. This is the Constellation Software model. Most people have not heard of Constellation Software. In 1995, Mark Leonard started it with no external funding and a conviction that patient, permanent ownership of software businesses was the most rational economic position available. He was right. Constellation today generates billions in annual revenue and operates hundreds of software companies across the world. The key word in that sentence is *operates*. They did not buy them and package them for resale. They run them.

*British* is not nationalism. It is specificity.

Software built for British people, by people who understand British life, will be better than software built generically and adapted. A tool for managing a British farm is not an American farm management tool with a pounds-sterling field substituted. The regulations differ. The terminology differs. The relationship between a British farmer and their land differs from anything an American product team has encountered. Getting this right requires starting with British reality, not adapting away from American defaults.

We are building the business that Constellation Software would have built if it had decided to focus on British consumer and SME life rather than enterprise vertical software — and if it had chosen to give its portfolio a public philosophical identity rather than remaining invisible.

## III. The Gap

Open your phone. Look at the applications you use to manage the serious, non-trivial parts of your life.

Your will. Your passport and the documents associated with it. Your insurance policies. Your mortgage. Your children's allowances and savings. Your farm, if you have one. Your health records. The documents your family would need to find in an emergency.

Now look at who made those applications.

Most of them were made in California. Some were made elsewhere in America. A few were made in Europe. Almost none were made in Britain, by people who understand British administrative life, and almost all of them were made by companies that have either already been acquired or are working toward that outcome.

This is not a feature of software in general. It is a feature of the specific type of software — consumer-facing, life-administration, privacy-sensitive — that nobody with the patience to build it properly has decided to focus on.

The gap is not lack of demand. British families have wills to store, documents to manage, farms to run, and money to organise. The demand is enormous and entirely real.

The gap is lack of attention.

Venture-backed companies pursue large addressable markets with rapid growth potential. A tool for helping British families organise their legal documents is not that. It is a slow, careful, trust-intensive product that takes years to build credibility and produces steady, durable revenue. It is exactly the kind of product the venture model cannot properly fund, and therefore the kind of product that does not currently exist at the quality it deserves.

We are not filling a gap that nobody noticed. We are filling a gap that the entire industry's incentive structure made it impossible to fill.

## IV. The Platform

Four products are a portfolio. Four products built on shared infrastructure are a platform. The difference between a portfolio and a platform is hundreds of millions of pounds in value, and more importantly, the difference between products that grow in isolation and products that grow each other.

We are building the platform quietly and in advance of the products that will need it.

**EazyAccess ID** is the identity layer. One account. One login. Every product. The switching cost of leaving any single EazyAccess product is modest. The switching cost of leaving EazyAccess when you use three products is significant. By the time a family uses five products, EazyAccess has become infrastructure. That is not a manipulation. It is the natural result of being useful across multiple domains of someone's life.

**EazyAccess Vault** is the encrypted storage layer. We built it for HushRealm, our private document vault. But the same infrastructure that stores your will can store the financial documents AllowanceGuard generates, the published reports PagePerfect produces, and the compliance certificates FarmCompanion manages. The Vault is the secure document layer for the entire platform. It is also, eventually, a service we can license to other organisations who need GDPR-compliant British document storage. Every product we build adds value to this infrastructure. The infrastructure adds value to every product we build.

**EazyAccess Pay** is the billing layer. One payment relationship, one statement, one family subscription that covers every product in the portfolio. The cross-product subscription — what we will call the EazyAccess Family Plan — is possible only because the billing is unified. This single commercial offering will eventually be our most important revenue driver, because users who adopt it stop evaluating individual products on price and start evaluating the platform on value.

**EazyAccess Acquire** is the organisational capability we are building to identify, purchase, redesign, and integrate existing British software companies into the platform. This is not a product. It is a process. The legal templates, integration playbooks, design processes, and cultural integration practices that allow us to add a £2 million ARR software business to the platform in six months and make it significantly better in twelve. Once developed, this capability is the engine of the entire long-term strategy.

## V. The Domains

We are starting with four products in three domains. We are planning for six domains and thirty-plus products over fifteen years.

**Life administration** is the first and most important domain. Every British family with a mortgage, a will, children, elderly parents, or anything of value eventually needs to organise the documents and decisions that hold their life together. When someone dies and the family cannot find the will, that is a failure of infrastructure. When a power of attorney needs to be established and nobody knows how to start, that is a failure of infrastructure. HushRealm and AllowanceGuard are the first tools in this domain. There are at least five more to build or acquire.

**British land and agriculture** is the second domain. FarmCompanion begins as a directory, but the full territory — farm management software, agricultural compliance tracking, rural property management, direct-to-consumer infrastructure for small farms — is large and almost entirely served by software that was not built for British agricultural reality. British farming is specific, regulated, and deeply embedded in community relationships that American products simply do not reflect.

**Professional document publishing** is the third. PagePerfect serves individuals initially, but British institutions — NHS trusts, local councils, housing associations, academic publishers, law firms — produce enormous volumes of formal documents that must be readable, properly formatted, and consistently produced. The institutional version of PagePerfect is a separate product from the consumer one, and both are needed.

**Small British business** is the fourth domain. There are millions of sole traders, independent retailers, tradespeople, and village businesses in Britain. They use QuickBooks and Xero and Sage — accounting systems built for American defaults that have been adapted for British use. The adaptation is imperfect. Building for British small business reality from the beginning produces a better product, and the EazyAccess platform already serves some of their document and compliance needs.

**British property** is the fifth. Landlords, homeowners, and property managers in Britain face regulatory obligations that increase in complexity every year. Energy efficiency compliance, tenancy documentation, maintenance records, conveyancing paperwork — this is an administrative infrastructure problem, and the software addressing it is fragmented, dated, and expensive.

**Health administration** is the sixth. Not clinical records — those are managed by the NHS. The organisational layer: storing your private consultations and prescriptions, tracking referrals and follow-ups, coordinating care for an elderly parent, managing health insurance claims. The NHS has built excellent clinical infrastructure. The administrative infrastructure that sits around it is largely unbuilt.

Together these six domains represent the administrative reality of British adult life. Not the glamorous parts. Not the parts that attract venture capital. The parts that matter when something goes wrong, when someone needs to find something, when a family is under stress and needs the practical foundations to hold.

## VI. A Letter to Founders

If you have built a software business serving one of these domains, or something adjacent, you may eventually receive an offer to sell.

Private equity will offer you a multiple. They will be friendly. They will explain the value of their network and their operational support. They may even be sincere about it. What they will not tell you — because it is not in their interest to — is that their fund has a defined lifespan, and your business is an asset within that fund, and the fund's objective is to return capital to its investors within that lifespan. What happens to your product after that is not their primary concern.

A strategic acquirer — a large technology company purchasing you to add to their portfolio — will offer you a higher multiple still. They may offer your team jobs. They may promise product continuity. Most of them mean it at the time of the offer. What happens is that your product becomes a feature, or it becomes a competitor they have neutralised, or it becomes a product abandoned quietly when it doesn't grow fast enough to justify resources within a company thirty times your size.

We are offering something different.

We will pay a fair multiple — typically three to five times annual recurring revenue for healthy businesses. We will not pay more than a business is worth, because we fund acquisitions from product revenue rather than from a pool of capital that needs to be deployed. This means we will sometimes lose an auction to a buyer with more money and shorter time horizons.

We will keep your product alive. Not as a feature of a larger platform, but as a distinct tool within a portfolio of distinct tools. We will invest in its design, its infrastructure, and its marketing. The EazyAccess brand mark on your product is not an absorption. It is an endorsement.

You may stay, if you want to. Many founders want to keep building the thing they started. We want that too, if you do. You may leave, if you have done what you came to do. We will run it well either way.

If what you have built is good, and you want it to survive you, talk to us.

## VII. The Numbers

We are not coy about the goal. We intend to build a business worth over one billion pounds.

The mathematics are not complicated.

A billion-pound valuation at standard software multiples requires roughly £80 to £100 million in annual recurring revenue. Reaching that level with individual products averaging £3 to £5 million in ARR — achievable for well-built, well-retained British software tools — requires twenty to thirty products in the portfolio.

Twenty to thirty products over fifteen years is not an aggressive target. It is a patient one.

The path looks like this.

In year three, four products generating modest revenue establish the platform and prove the model. Revenue is small. What is not small is the infrastructure: the ID layer, the Vault, the billing system, the brand. Those take time to build and are the foundation of everything else.

By year six, the first acquisitions have been integrated. The platform serves ten products. Revenue is in the range of £10 to £15 million annually. The organisation is profitable. It funds its own growth.

By year ten, twenty products and a cross-product family subscription produce revenue approaching £50 million. The EazyAccess brand is known to British consumers in the way that Sage is known — a trusted name in practical software — but with a design standard Sage never achieved and a user relationship Sage never cared to build.

By year fifteen, the portfolio is a British institution. Thirty products. Revenue above £100 million. A valuation that reflects what it actually cost in patience and craft to build something that deserved to be worth that much.

We are not in a hurry. We are serious.

## VIII. Six Decisions Made in Advance

Most organisations describe their values on a wall. Values on a wall change when they become inconvenient. We prefer decisions made in advance, publicly, so that they can be tested against our actions rather than our intentions.

Here are ours.

**We do not use dark patterns.** Manufactured urgency, hidden cancellation flows, confusing pricing structures, interfaces designed to extract rather than serve — none of it. If a product is genuinely useful, people will choose to pay for it. If they wouldn't choose to without manipulation, the product is not good enough yet.

**We build for British life.** Not British by registration. British in substance. Our products know what a council tax bill is, what the NHS looks like from the patient's side, what a tenancy agreement requires under English and Scottish law, what a British farm's regulatory obligations actually are. When context matters, we get it right.

**We own what we build.** Permanently. We do not build things to sell them. We do not acquire things to package them. Every product we own today we expect to be improving twenty years from now. If we would not own it for twenty years, we do not build it.

**We design as if design is the product.** Because it is. A confusing interface is not an aesthetic failure. It is a functional failure. Time a user spends confused is time they spent experiencing our product failing them. We treat the design of every product as seriously as we treat the code.

**Privacy is the infrastructure, not the pitch.** We do not advertise our privacy practices as a feature. We implement them as a requirement and mention them once. User data is not revenue. Encryption is not optional. GDPR compliance is the floor. We do not measure our practices against the legal minimum. We measure them against what a user would consider fair if they read every line of our implementation.

**We remove more than we add.** Feature requests are easy to receive and easy to implement. The discipline is knowing which ones to refuse. Our products do fewer things than our competitors. They do those things better. We prefer to do one thing that earns trust over three things that dilute focus. When we feel pressure to add features, we slow down and ask whether we are solving a real problem or avoiding the harder work of making the existing product excellent.

## IX. The One Decision That Makes Everything Else Possible

This document describes a strategy that requires a particular kind of patience. Patient capital. Patient development. Patient acquisition. Compounding slowly enough that the results are durable.

None of it is possible if EazyAccess takes outside investment.

Not because investors are bad. Not because the relationships are unpleasant. Because the structure changes everything. One investor introduces one timeline. One timeline introduces one objective that is not the product. The moment that objective exists, every decision is made in its shadow.

Constellation Software never took outside capital. 37signals never took outside capital. The most enduring software companies in the world — the ones that got better every year, that their customers could rely on a decade after they first signed up — were almost uniformly built on revenue.

Revenue is slow. Revenue requires building something people want badly enough to pay for it. Revenue means you cannot fake traction. Revenue means every product decision is tested against whether users find it worth their money.

Revenue is the right constraint.

We have chosen it permanently. Not as a temporary position we hold until the right offer arrives. As the founding architecture of the organisation.

## X. Who This Is For

If you are a potential customer, we want you to know that the product you pay for today will exist in ten years. It will be better in ten years. Nobody will sell it, pivot it, sunset it, or hollow it out in pursuit of metrics that have nothing to do with whether you found it useful. That is the promise we are able to make because of how we are structured, not because of how much we mean it.

If you are a founder thinking about what you want to do with the thing you built, we want you to know that permanence is possible. You do not have to sell to someone who will eventually close it. You do not have to raise until you are so large that an IPO is the only exit available. There is a third option: sell to someone who will run it as well as you did and better. We intend to be that someone.

If you are a developer, designer, or product thinker who is tired of building things that get acquired, changed, shut down, or compromised — EazyAccess is being built for you to work at. Not a company optimised for its exit. A company optimised for its products.

If you are reading this as a potential competitor or as someone who thinks this model is naive — we understand why. The venture capital model works. It produces significant returns. It has funded genuinely important companies. Our argument is not that it is wrong in general. Our argument is that it is wrong for this particular type of software, serving this particular population, over this particular time horizon. Patient, permanent ownership produces better British consumer software than exit-seeking ownership. We believe that completely and intend to prove it.

---

The name EazyAccess makes a specific promise. Not that building the company is easy — it is not. That using the products should be. That the part of your life they touch should require less effort, less confusion, and less anxiety than it did before.

That promise takes a long time to fulfil properly.

We have the time.
